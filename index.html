This layer displays the location of 21 drinking water intake facilities across Ontario and the average nitrogen and phosphorus values measured in 2019. These sampling locations regularly test the water for various chemicals and contaminants throughout the year, including ammonia, chloride, and chlorophyll. For our risk analysis, we identified phosphorus and nitrogen as chemicals of interest due to their role in eutrophication.

Eutrophication is the process of nutrient accumulation, primarily phosphorus and nitrogen, in aquatic ecosystems. This leads to the increased growth of algae and plant matter, resulting in harmful algal blooms (HAB) and low-oxygen (hypoxic) waters. These conditions have adverse effects on marine life and can negatively impact human health. These adverse effects include respiratory problems, rashes, and stomach or liver illnesses.

This layer displays the location of 38 air quality monitoring stations in Ontario and the average PM2.5 values measured in 2022. Fine particulate matter, or PM2.5, is an air pollutant that is either emitted from external sources (primary particles) or formed in the atmosphere through chemical processes (secondary particles). Short-term exposure to PM2.5 has been associated with premature mortality, increased hospital admissions, acute and chronic bronchitis, and asthma attacks.

This layer displays a comprehensive measure of environmental risk for 573 census subdivisions in Ontario. The above environmental indicators (nitrogen and phosphorus levels, PM2.5 levels, and number of contaminated sites per CSD) were synthesized into a single measure to present individual risk scores for each census subdivision. Each environmental factor was categorized into quartiles, with numerical scores assigned based on their quartile ranking. This data was assessed using two methods: a combined risk score and a scaled combined risk score. The combined risk score for each CSD was then calculated by summing up the individual risk scores across the selected environmental indicators.

The scaled risk score offers an alternative assessment. Like the combined risk score, each environmental indicator was categorized into quartiles. However the resulting values were scaled between 1 and 4, providing a different perspective on overall risk.

These two measures — scaled and non-scaled risk scores — allow for the display of two separate maps and offer a comprehensive understanding of environmental risk in Ontario. The non-scaled score provides a raw assessment of risk levels calculated through each indicator, while the scaled risk score makes the data more interpretable and comparable between census subdivisions.

Users can toggle census subdivisions on and off by on combining varying layers in different ways. This allows users to gain a deeper understanding of how the layers are related to each other in our assessment. More specifically, this interactive feature enables users to easily identify areas that may require targeted interventions to ensure environmental justice for vulnerable populations.

There are two buttons, that when clicked, scroll through the available layer options. The displayed label indicates the layer that will be selected for assessment.

Below each of these buttons are items that are selectable by a button click. An item is selected when it is coloured. The colours correspond to quartile values, as shown in the layer legend, with the lowest to highest values displayed from left to right. When an item is selected, it indicates that all census subdivisons within that quartile for the layer indicated by the above button should be chosen in the assesment.

There is also a button indicating how to combine the chosen subdivisions. Selecting "AND" means a subdivision should be in the quartiles of both layers, whereas selecting "OR" requires it to be only in one. Finally, there is a "run" button which will display the data as selected by the inputs. To redo the assessment, simply click the same button again, modify the input, and run once more!

Please be patient, as it may take a moment to run.
